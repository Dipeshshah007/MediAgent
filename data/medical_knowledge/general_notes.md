## Triage urgency principles (general)

Cases are commonly grouped by urgency. Emergency presentations include signs
such as chest pain, severe difficulty breathing, sudden weakness or speech
difficulty, heavy bleeding, loss of consciousness, or thoughts of self-harm.
These warrant immediate emergency care rather than routine assessment.
Urgent-but-not-emergency cases may need same-day professional review. Routine
cases are mild, stable, and often self-limiting.

## Common upper respiratory infection (general overview)

Self-limiting viral upper respiratory infections often present with sore
throat, runny or blocked nose, mild fever, cough, and fatigue lasting several
days to about two weeks. Supportive care such as rest, fluids, and
over-the-counter symptom relief is typical. Warning signs that merit
professional review include high persistent fever, difficulty breathing,
severe or worsening pain, or symptoms lasting beyond the expected window.

## Dehydration (general overview)

Dehydration can cause thirst, dry mouth, reduced urination, dark urine,
fatigue, dizziness, and in more severe cases confusion or rapid heartbeat.
Mild dehydration is usually managed with oral fluids. Severe dehydration,
especially with confusion or inability to keep fluids down, needs prompt
professional attention.

## Tension-type headache (general overview)

Tension-type headaches commonly present as a dull, band-like pressure on both
sides of the head, often linked to stress, poor sleep, or screen time. They
are usually managed with rest, hydration, and over-the-counter analgesia.
Sudden severe headache, headache with fever and stiff neck, headache after
head injury, or headache with neurological changes are warning signs that need
urgent assessment.

## Medication safety principles (general)

Combining multiple medications can increase the risk of interactions.
General caution is warranted when combining blood-thinning agents with
anti-inflammatory drugs, when stacking multiple sedating medicines, or when
adding a new drug to an existing regimen. A pharmacist or prescriber should
review any real medication list; automated tools are only a prompt to ask a
professional, never a substitute.

## General next-step guidance

For most non-emergency presentations, reasonable next steps include monitoring
symptoms, supportive self-care, and seeking professional review if symptoms
worsen, persist beyond the expected course, or new warning signs appear.
